## Reference sources (verified by spot-check; honest about each)

In order of practical usefulness:

1. **Your own field experience** — for species you've harvested in Ithaca over multiple years, this is the gold standard. Higher signal than any external source for those species.
2. **USA-NPN data viewer** — https://data.usanpn.org/observations/ — empirical median DOY; noisy but real. Per `npn-validation-NE-frost-anchored.md`, expect ~14d leading-edge bias (volunteers flag "ripe" at first-color, not harvest peak). Apply the offset before using p10.
3. **USDA NRCS Plant Guides** — https://plants.sc.egov.usda.gov/ — PDF Plant Guides exist for *some* species at predictable paths but coverage is partial. Page is JS-rendered so look up case-by-case, not programmatically. Conservative on dates; useful as a citation backstop.
4. **Wikipedia** — CC BY-SA 4.0. Phenology info is typically vague ("summer flowers", "fall fruit"). Useful for identification and species existence in a region; weak for DOY precision.
5. **GoBotany / Native Plant Trust** — https://gobotany.nativeplanttrust.org/ — New England regional database, ~3,000 species. URL pattern `/species/genus/species/`. Whether they expose structured phenology fields is unconfirmed; worth investigating before relying on it.

**Sources I considered and dropped:**
- ❌ **Cornell Cooperative Extension** — coverage skews to commercial crops (apples, grapes, vegetables); little evidence they publish harvest calendars for foraged species. Useful case-by-case for cultivars only.
- ❌ **Eat the Weeds (Green Deane)** — confirmed Florida-based; his "elderberry blossoms any time of year" comes from Florida and doesn't translate to Ithaca. Useful only for identification, not date precision.
- ❌ **PFAF (Plants For A Future)** — CC BY-NC-SA license is incompatible with our paid tier. Use as a research reference for your own synthesis only; don't ingest fields directly.
