# Proposed Tier 4: Subtropical, Mediterranean, and tropical edibles

The current catalog is heavily Northeast-temperate — even after the
Tier 1-3 expansion. San Diego's public-tree data alone has ~10,000
pins of edibles we don't track today. Same gap in LA, Phoenix, Miami,
Tampa, New Orleans, Honolulu — all in the public dataset.

This list closes the climate gap. Format same as Tier 1-3:
**Scientific name — Common name (forage parts)**.

## Tier 4A — high coverage in city tree data, low foraging risk

These are widely planted as public street/park trees and have clear
edibility and clear ID. Foraging them is unambiguous.

### Citrus (8)
- *Citrus sinensis* — Sweet orange (fruit)
- *Citrus limon* — Lemon (fruit)
- *Citrus reticulata* — Mandarin / tangerine (fruit)
- *Citrus paradisi* — Grapefruit (fruit)
- *Citrus aurantium* — Sour orange / Seville orange (fruit) — preserves, marmalade
- *Citrus medica* — Citron (fruit, peel) — confitures
- *Fortunella japonica* — Kumquat (fruit) — eaten whole, peel and all
- *Citrus sp.* — Citrus (unspecified) — for catch-all genus matches in city data

### Mediterranean / fruit-bearing landscape trees (8)
- *Persea americana* — Avocado (fruit)
- *Olea europaea* — Olive (fruit) — must be cured before eating
- *Ficus carica* — Common fig (fruit) — distinct from ornamental Ficus
- *Punica granatum* — Pomegranate (fruit, seed)
- *Eriobotrya japonica* — Loquat (fruit) — early-spring fruiter
- *Diospyros kaki* — Japanese persimmon (fruit) — fuyu (eat firm) or hachiya (eat ripe-soft)
- *Ceratonia siliqua* — Carob (pod) — ground for chocolate substitute, sweet pulp
- *Laurus nobilis* — Bay laurel (leaf) — culinary herb

### Date palms + similar (3)
- *Phoenix dactylifera* — Date palm (fruit)
- *Phoenix canariensis* — Canary Island date palm (fruit) — small, fibrous, foragable
- *Phoenix sp.* — Date palm (unspecified)

### Cactus (1)
- *Opuntia ficus-indica* — Prickly pear / Indian fig (fruit, pad) — fruit (tunas) and young pads (nopales) both eaten

**Tier 4A total: 20**

## Tier 4B — Florida, Gulf Coast, Hawaii, true tropics

Less common in the cities our public dataset covers (most Dryad cities
are temperate/subtropical, not true tropical), but worth including for
users in Miami, Tampa, Honolulu, southern coastal areas.

- *Mangifera indica* — Mango (fruit)
- *Litchi chinensis* — Lychee (fruit)
- *Annona cherimola* — Cherimoya (fruit)
- *Annona squamosa* — Sugar apple (fruit)
- *Psidium guajava* — Common guava (fruit)
- *Acca sellowiana* — Pineapple guava / feijoa (fruit, flower)
- *Casimiroa edulis* — White sapote (fruit)
- *Eugenia uniflora* — Surinam cherry (fruit)
- *Carissa macrocarpa* — Natal plum (fruit) — common SoCal hedge
- *Coccoloba uvifera* — Sea grape (fruit) — coastal SE
- *Carica papaya* — Papaya (fruit)
- *Macadamia integrifolia* — Macadamia (nut) — California + Hawaii
- *Macadamia tetraphylla* — Macadamia (nut)

**Tier 4B total: 13**

## Tier 4C — pepper trees, niche

- *Schinus molle* — California / Peruvian pepper tree (drupe) — traditional source of "pink peppercorns"
- *Schinus terebinthifolia* — Brazilian pepper tree (drupe) — sold commercially as pink peppercorns BUT FDA has issued advisories; can cause GI / dermatitis in sensitive people. **I'd add with strong caution notes or skip.**
- *Yucca baccata* — Banana yucca (fruit, flower) — Southwest desert
- *Yucca filamentosa* — Yucca (flower) — flowers edible; fruit usually doesn't form in cultivation

**Tier 4C total: 4**

## Deliberate omissions / hold-outs

- **Ornamental Ficus** (F. microcarpa, F. macrophylla, F. rubiginosa) — technically edible but the fruit is small, fibrous, and not pleasant. Listing them risks people foraging the wrong fig. **Stick to Ficus carica only.**
- **Eucalyptus** — not human-edible. (Honey from eucalyptus is fine but not pin-worthy.)
- **Nerium oleander** (oleander) — TOXIC. Not edible at all despite being widely planted.
- **Liquidambar** — sweetgum resin chewable but very niche. Skip.
- **Cinnamomum camphora** — camphor tree, not edible; the cinnamon spice comes from a different species.

## Grand totals

- Tier 4A (high-confidence common): **20**
- Tier 4B (tropical regional): **13**
- Tier 4C (niche / cautioned): **4**
- **Total proposed: 37**
- Combined with current 109 → **146-species catalog**

## Match rate impact

For San Diego's 212k tree inventory, current match rate is 3.6%.
Adding Tier 4A alone would bring it to roughly 8-10% (roughly
doubling visible pins). Adding all three tiers, probably ~12%.
LA, Phoenix, and the Florida cities would see similar jumps.

## Recommended path

I'd ship **all of 4A + most of 4B** (tropical fruit trees with clear
edibility), **drop *Schinus terebinthifolia*** entirely (FDA caution
on the species; hard to recommend), keep S. molle as more
traditional. Yucca species I'd add at the species level rather than
as a genus catch-all — flowers are the part most reliably foraged
across species.
